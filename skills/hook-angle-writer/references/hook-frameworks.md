# Hook and Angle Frameworks (Reference)

This reference is a concise, original restatement of common direct-response frameworks for writing hooks, headlines, and angles. It contains no book text. Use it as the working library behind the `hook-angle-writer` skill.

An angle is the underlying idea (which desire, which promise, which enemy, which mechanism). A hook is the specific line that grabs attention and pulls the reader into the next line. A headline is the hook that sits at the top of an ad, page, email, or post. One angle can spawn many hooks. Always separate the two.

---

## 1. Hook, Story, Offer

The core sequence for most persuasive copy and content:

1. **Hook** - stop the scroll and earn the next few seconds. It promises a payoff, opens a curiosity gap, or names a felt problem.
2. **Story** - build belief and emotion. Show the transformation, the mechanism, the proof, the relatable struggle.
3. **Offer** - tell them exactly what to do next and why now.

Rules of thumb:

- The hook only has one job: win the next line. It does not have to sell.
- The story earns the right to make the offer.
- If the hook overpromises what the story and offer cannot deliver, trust breaks. Keep hooks honest.
- Every asset (ad, email, reel, page section, podcast clip) can be mapped to this sequence.

---

## 2. Role of the headline and its four qualities

The headline is the ad for the ad. Its only job is to get the first sentence read, which gets the next sentence read, and so on. Most readers decide from the headline alone whether to continue.

A strong headline usually carries one or more of these four qualities:

1. **Self-interest** - promises a clear benefit the reader wants. Answers "what is in it for me."
2. **News** - offers something new, improved, or newly possible. Novelty earns attention.
3. **Curiosity** - opens a gap the reader needs to close, without being vague or tricky.
4. **Quick and easy** - signals the result comes fast and without heavy effort or risk.

The strongest headlines combine self-interest with one of the others (news + benefit, or curiosity + benefit). Curiosity alone can pull clicks but under-converts if it is not tied to a real desire.

## 3. Headline template patterns

Proven skeletons to adapt. Fill with the avatar's real desire, the offer's real mechanism, and the brand's real voice. Never ship the raw template.

- **How to ...** - "How to [get desired result] without [pain or sacrifice]."
- **Who else wants ...** - "Who else wants [desired result]?" Implies others already have it (social proof baked in).
- **Warning ...** - "Warning: [risk the reader did not know about]." Loss-framed, pattern-interrupting.
- **At last ...** - "At last, [solution] that [does the hard thing]." Signals relief and news.
- **The secret of / secrets of ...** - "The secret of [result] that [group] uses." Curiosity + authority.
- **Now you can ...** - "Now you can [do desired thing] even if [common obstacle]." News + possibility.
- **They didn't think I could, but I did ...** - underdog proof story hook. Great for founder or attractive-character angles.
- **Are you ...** - "Are you [making this mistake / suffering this problem]?" Self-identification hook.

Other reliable skeletons: "Give me [short time] and I will [result]," "[Number] ways to [result]," "The lie you were told about [topic]," "If you [situation], read this."

Pattern selection by awareness (see section 8): unaware and problem-aware audiences respond to Warning, Are you, and problem-naming hooks; solution-aware audiences respond to How to, At last, Now you can, Secrets of; most-aware audiences respond to direct offer, price, and urgency hooks.

## 4. Life-Force 8 (biological desires)

Eight hardwired human wants that require no teaching. Copy that ties an offer to one of these desires pulls harder than copy that argues features. Choose the one or two that genuinely fit the offer.

1. Survival, life extension, health, and vitality
2. Enjoyment of food and drink
3. Freedom from fear, pain, and danger
4. Sexual companionship
5. Comfortable living conditions
6. To be superior, winning, keeping up
7. Care and protection of loved ones
8. Social approval and status

**Secondary (learned) wants** that layer on top: to be informed, curiosity, cleanliness, efficiency, convenience, reliability and quality, beauty and style, economy and profit, bargains.

Usage: pick the dominant desire the offer actually serves, write the angle from that desire, then let secondary wants sharpen the specifics. Do not staple a desire that the product cannot honestly satisfy.

## 5. PAS - Problem, Agitate, Solve

A compact persuasion arc, strong for cold and problem-aware audiences.

1. **Problem** - name the specific pain the reader feels. Be concrete, use their words.
2. **Agitate** - make the cost of the problem vivid: what it keeps costing, how it worsens, how it feels. Do not manufacture fear beyond the truth.
3. **Solve** - present the mechanism and offer as the relief.

Works as a full ad, an email, a page section, or a single hook (a PAS hook front-loads problem + agitation and teases the solve).

---

## 6. Before, After, Bridge

A transformation frame that sells the gap and the path across it.

1. **Before** - the reader's current painful or stuck state.
2. **After** - the desired state once the problem is gone. Make it specific and emotional.
3. **Bridge** - the offer or mechanism that carries them from Before to After.

Good for hooks that lead with the After ("Imagine [after state]"), or with the contrast ("You are [before]. You could be [after]. Here is the bridge.").

---

## 7. If / Then

A qualification and promise structure.

- **If [reader's situation or desire], then [specific promise or next step].**

It self-selects the right reader ("If you run a gym and hate chasing leads ...") and pairs a condition with a payoff. Useful for targeting a segment inside a broad audience and for calls to action.

---

## 8. Awareness levels (traffic temperature)

Match the hook to how much the reader already knows. Getting this wrong is the most common reason a good hook fails.

- **Cold / unaware or problem-aware** - does not know you and may not name the problem. Lead with the problem, a pattern interrupt, a story, or a big curiosity gap. Avoid product names and jargon.
- **Warm / solution-aware** - knows solutions like yours exist, is comparing. Lead with the unique mechanism, differentiation, proof, and "how to get X without Y."
- **Hot / product-aware or most-aware** - knows you and the offer. Lead with the offer, price, bonuses, guarantee, scarcity, and a direct call to action.

Tag every hook with the awareness level it is written for so it is not deployed to the wrong audience.

---

## 9. Curiosity and fascination bullets

Short, punchy lines that tease a specific payoff without revealing it, driving the reader to keep going, open, or buy. Each bullet names an intriguing outcome or secret and withholds the how.

Reliable bullet openers:

- "The [adjective] reason [surprising fact] ..."
- "What never to do when [situation] ..."
- "How to know if [condition] in [short time] ..."
- "Why [common belief] is costing you [consequence] ..."
- "The [number]-second [action] that [benefit] ..."
- "A little-known way to [result] even if [obstacle] ..."
- "The truth about [topic] that [authority] will not tell you ..."

Rules:

- Every bullet must be backed by a real payoff in the story or offer. No empty tease.
- Specificity beats hype. Numbers, timeframes, and concrete nouns outperform adjectives.
- Cluster bullets to stack curiosity; they work in ads, emails, page sections, and video scripts.

---

## 10. Channel adaptation

The same angle is re-cut for each surface. Keep the angle; change the shape.

- **Ad (paid)** - hook in the first line and first second, tight, one clear desire, one clear next step. Assume no prior context.
- **Email subject line** - short, curiosity or benefit forward, honest to the body, avoids spammy words and false promises. Pair with a preview line.
- **Social / organic post** - native to the platform, first line is the hook, story-led, softer sell.
- **Funnel header / page hero** - the promise headline plus subhead; matches the ad that drove the click (message match) and the awareness level of the traffic.
- **Podcast or video clip** - a spoken hook in the first few seconds, curiosity or contrarian statement, sets up the story beat that follows.

---

## Honesty and risk notes

- A hook may dramatize a real benefit; it may not promise a result the offer cannot deliver.
- Flag any income, health, financial, or legal claim in a hook for confirmation and possible disclaimer.
- Avoid fake scarcity, fake countdowns, and clickbait curiosity that the content does not pay off. These win a click and lose the customer.

## 11. Hormozi Ad Hook Taxonomy

From $100M Leads. A deeper hook classification system for video and image ads. Use this to generate diverse creative variants for testing.

### Verbal Hooks (what you say)

1. **Labels:** call out a specific group by name. "Clark County moms." "Agency owners with 5-20 employees." Labels make the viewer self-identify immediately — "that's me" — and earn the next 3 seconds.

2. **Yes-Questions:** ask a question where "yes" means they have the problem. "Wake up to pee more than once a night?" "Spend more time managing your tools than managing your clients?" The yes self-qualifies them and creates a micro-commitment.

3. **If-Then statements:** "If you [specific situation], then [specific promise or consequence]." "If your agency runs on 4+ tools, then you're losing 10 hours a week." Combines qualification with a bold claim.

4. **Ridiculous Results:** lead with a result so specific or extreme it forces attention. "I replaced my $300 skincare cream with a $39 peptide complex." "I went from 2 to 28 gym locations in 28 months." The specificity and extremity earn the watch.

### Non-Verbal Hooks (what you show)

1. **Contrast:** visual disruption through color, movement, or unexpected change. A presenter in a neon green shirt in a grey office. A sudden cut from stillness to motion. Contrast breaks the scroll pattern visually before the verbal hook lands.

2. **Resemblance:** "look like a duck to catch ducks." Make the ad look like native content the avatar already watches. A UGC-style phone video for a B2C product. A LinkedIn text post for a B2B service. The ad blends into the feed because it resembles what the avatar already consumes.

3. **The Scene:** a visual yes-question. Show a scene the avatar recognizes — a desk covered in sticky notes, a phone with 47 unread Slack messages, a closet full of unworn clothes. The visual alone makes them say "that's me" before a word is spoken.

### The Cocktail Party Effect

People filter out most stimuli but tune in when they hear their name (or something personally relevant). Hooks work the same way: a specific label, a specific pain, or a specific desire cuts through the noise because the brain recognizes it as "for me."

**Application:** the more specific the hook (exact avatar, exact pain, exact desire), the harder it cuts through. Generic hooks ("want to grow your business?") get filtered. Specific hooks ("agency owners losing clients to late deliverables") get noticed.

### The What-Who-When Value Framework

From $100M Leads. A framework for extending a hook into a full ad body by layering three value dimensions:

- **What (the value equation mapped to ad copy):**
  - Dream Outcome ↔ Nightmare (what they want vs. what they fear)
  - Perceived Success ↔ Risk (how likely it works vs. what could go wrong)
  - Time-to-Goal ↔ Speed (how fast they get there)
  - Effort/Sacrifice ↔ Ease (how hard vs. how easy you make it)

- **Who (status dynamics):** who else benefits or judges when they succeed — spouse, kids, rivals, coworkers, competitors. Frame the result as a status shift, not just a practical improvement.

- **When (timeline visualization):** past-present-future. Show them the before (past pain), the now (current frustration), and the after (future state with your offer). The contrast makes the gap vivid and the bridge desirable.
