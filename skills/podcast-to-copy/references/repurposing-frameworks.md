# Podcast Repurposing Frameworks Reference

This reference summarizes the transcript-mining and copy-repurposing frameworks used by the `podcast-to-copy` skill in original wording. It is a field guide for extracting usable marketing assets from long-form recordings. It is not a source of client facts. Always verify the transcript and carry risk flags forward.

## Core Principle

A long-form recording is a raw asset mine. The work is to find the reusable pieces, preserve their source, and shape them for the right channel without changing the substance.

Good repurposing follows this order:

1. Store or link the raw transcript.
2. Read the full transcript.
3. Extract reusable assets with location markers.
4. Flag risky or unverified claims.
5. Build finished copy from the assets.
6. Propose wiki updates for avatar and character profiles.

Do not start by writing captions. Start by mining the material.

## The Eleven Asset Types

### 1. Hooks

Hooks are lines or ideas that make the audience want the next sentence. In transcripts, hooks often appear as:

- a surprising claim
- a sharp warning
- a vivid problem statement
- a counterintuitive lesson
- a compressed before and after
- a line that names the audience's private frustration

A hook does not need to sell the whole offer. Its job is to open a clip, post, email, or page section.

Capture:

- exact or near-exact wording
- why it stops attention
- likely channel: clip, social, email subject, page headline
- awareness level: cold, warm, hot

### 2. Stories

Stories are sequences with a setup, tension, action, and outcome. They are stronger than abstract advice because the audience can see change happen.

Capture:

- who the story is about
- where it starts
- what problem or conflict appears
- what changed
- what lesson the story proves
- where it could be used: email, social post, sales page, character profile

Do not turn a vague example into a full story. If the transcript only gives a fragment, label it as a fragment.

### 3. Parables

A parable is a short story used to teach a broader lesson. In marketing, parables are useful because they make a belief memorable.

Capture:

- the simple plot
- the lesson
- the belief it supports
- the enemy or false belief it undermines
- the offer or mechanism it can lead into

A parable can be from the speaker's life, a client example, a field observation, or a comparison. Keep it truthful to what was said.

### 4. Quotes

Quotes are lines that can stand alone in the speaker's voice. They may become quote graphics, email openings, short posts, page callouts, or clip captions.

Capture quotes when they are:

- concise
- vivid
- specific
- emotionally charged
- unusually phrased
- clearly in the speaker's voice

Do not put quotation marks around a heavily rewritten line. If polished, label it as a paraphrase.

### 5. Contrarian Beliefs

Contrarian beliefs push against conventional wisdom. They are powerful because they create polarity and curiosity.

Capture beliefs that sound like:

- "Most people get this backward"
- "The real problem is not X, it is Y"
- "The thing everyone recommends is actually the trap"
- "You do not need more X, you need Y"

For each belief, capture:

- the conventional belief being challenged
- the speaker's replacement belief
- the reasoning or proof offered
- the risk level if the claim touches health, income, finance, legal, or credentials

### 6. Enemies

Enemies are the villains in the speaker's worldview. They do not need to be people. Often they are systems, bad advice, myths, habits, institutions, or false beliefs.

Common enemy types:

- the broken system
- the lazy shortcut
- the misleading expert
- the false metric
- the outdated method
- the fear that keeps the audience stuck
- the status quo

Enemies create "us vs them" energy for attractive-character work. Use them carefully. Avoid defamatory claims or unsupported accusations.

### 7. Epiphanies

Epiphanies are turning-point realizations. They explain how the speaker came to believe what they believe now.

Capture moments like:

- "That was when I realized..."
- "I had been looking at it wrong"
- "The missing piece was..."
- "Everything changed when..."

For each epiphany, capture:

- what the speaker believed before
- what happened
- what they realized
- what belief or mechanism it supports now

Epiphanies are especially useful for emails, founder stories, and sales-page mechanism sections.

### 8. Proof

Proof is anything that makes a claim more believable. Proof can be formal or informal, but it must be represented accurately.

Proof types include:

- numbers and results
- named case studies
- demonstrations
- before and after descriptions
- customer or patient stories
- credentials and experience
- third-party recognition
- process evidence
- repeated observations from the speaker's work

Flag proof if it lacks context, documentation, timeframe, denominator, or approval. Health, income, financial, legal, and credential proof needs extra review.

### 9. Objections

Objections are the doubts and pushbacks that could stop the audience from believing, engaging, or buying.

Transcript objections can appear as:

- questions from a host or guest
- "people always ask me..."
- "I used to think..."
- "the problem with this is..."
- cost, time, risk, effort, identity, trust, or complexity concerns

Capture both the objection and the speaker's answer. Map strong objection handling to future sales pages, emails, and offer documents.

### 10. Product Claims

Product claims are specific statements about what a product, service, framework, supplement, method, program, or offer does.

Capture:

- the exact claim
- what product or method it refers to
- the audience or use case
- the proof offered
- any conditions or limitations stated
- risk level

Do not make product claims stronger than the transcript supports. If the speaker says "may help," do not rewrite it as "will fix."

### 11. Content Pillars

Content pillars are recurring themes the speaker can own repeatedly. They are not one-off lines. They are categories that could support many posts, clips, emails, and talks.

A good content pillar has:

- a clear theme
- repeated evidence in the transcript
- audience relevance
- room for many examples
- connection to the character, avatar, and offer

Common pillar types:

- enemy or myth pillar
- mechanism pillar
- proof and case-study pillar
- personal journey pillar
- objection-answer pillar
- practical how-to pillar
- worldview or values pillar

## Output Mapping

Use the mined asset type to choose the right output.

| Asset type | Best outputs |
|---|---|
| Hooks | titles, clip opens, subject lines, social openings, page headlines |
| Stories | emails, posts, founder story, webinar sections |
| Parables | belief-shift emails, social posts, sales-page sections |
| Quotes | quote graphics, captions, post opens, page callouts |
| Contrarian beliefs | titles, hooks, email angles, content pillars, page mechanism |
| Enemies | attractive character, polarity posts, campaign themes |
| Epiphanies | nurture emails, origin story, mechanism explanation |
| Proof | sales-page proof, offer credibility, emails, social proof posts |
| Objections | FAQ, sales-page objection handling, email sequence, offer refinement |
| Product claims | offer pages, compliance review list, launch emails |
| Content pillars | content calendar, podcast themes, clip strategy |

## Podcast Description Formula

Create two versions.

Short description:

```text
In this episode, [speaker] explains [main hook or problem] for [avatar]. You'll learn [takeaway 1], [takeaway 2], and [takeaway 3], including [proof/story/contrarian belief].
```

Long description:

1. Open with the strongest hook or problem.
2. State who the episode is for.
3. Summarize the core conversation in plain language.
4. List 3 to 6 specific takeaways.
5. Include one proof point or story if verified.
6. Close with the next step or soft call to action.

Keep descriptions honest to the transcript. Do not promise a takeaway that was not covered.

## Title Generation Patterns

Use multiple title angles so the client can choose by channel and audience awareness.

- **Curiosity:** "The overlooked reason [problem] keeps happening"
- **Benefit:** "How to [result] without [pain]"
- **Contrarian:** "Why [common advice] may be making [problem] worse"
- **Enemy:** "The [myth/system/habit] keeping [avatar] stuck"
- **Story:** "What [event/story] taught [speaker] about [lesson]"
- **Proof:** "What [result/example] reveals about [mechanism]"
- **Question:** "Is [assumption] the real reason [problem]?"

A title can be punchy, but it must not exceed what the episode pays off.

## Social Post Patterns

### Story Post

1. Open with the moment of tension.
2. Give just enough context.
3. Show the turn.
4. State the lesson.
5. Apply it to the audience.
6. End with a question or light call to action.

### Contrarian Belief Post

1. Name the common belief.
2. Explain why it fails.
3. Replace it with the speaker's belief.
4. Add proof or reasoning.
5. Tell the reader what to do differently.

### Quote Expansion Post

1. Lead with the quote or paraphrase.
2. Explain what it means.
3. Give one example.
4. Tie it to the avatar's problem.
5. End with a practical takeaway.

### Parable Post

1. Tell the short illustrative story.
2. Name the lesson.
3. Connect it to the audience's stuck point.
4. Show the better path.

## Email Patterns

### One-Idea Episode Email

- Subject: hook from the transcript
- Opening: story, quote, or problem
- Body: one lesson from the episode
- Proof: transcript-sourced example
- CTA: listen, reply, book, read, or take the next step

### Epiphany Email

- Before belief
- Moment that challenged it
- New belief
- Why it matters to the reader
- CTA tied to the offer or content

### Objection Email

- Name the objection in the audience's language
- Validate why it makes sense
- Reframe using the speaker's explanation
- Give proof or an example
- CTA to the next step

Use one main idea per email. Do not cram the whole episode into one message.

## Sales-Page Angle Mapping

Use transcript assets to feed page sections:

- **Headline:** hook, contrarian belief, or dream-outcome line
- **Problem section:** enemy, objection, pain, or false belief
- **Mechanism section:** epiphany, contrarian belief, process explanation
- **Proof section:** verified results, case studies, credentials, demonstrations
- **Objection section:** objections and the speaker's answers
- **Offer tie-in:** product claims and the mechanism, only if approved
- **FAQ:** objections and conditions from the transcript
- **Callout boxes:** strong quotes or sharp paraphrases

Sales-page copy carries more claim risk than organic content. Preserve review notes.

## Avatar Update Signals

A transcript can reveal audience research when the speaker names what people want, fear, misunderstand, or say out loud.

Add proposed avatar insights when the transcript reveals:

- exact customer language
- recurring pains
- desired outcomes
- fears and objections
- identity statements
- failed alternatives
- buying triggers
- misconceptions
- awareness level

Do not overwrite the avatar silently. Propose sourced additions.

## Attractive-Character Update Signals

A transcript can enrich the speaker's character profile when it reveals real personal material.

Add proposed character updates when the transcript reveals:

- backstory beats
- flaws or humanizing details
- parables
- enemies and polarity
- core message language
- signature phrases
- repeated worldview
- origin of the method
- visual or behavioral cues

Do not invent biography to make the character more dramatic. Source everything.

## Risk Flag Rules

Flag before publishing when an asset includes:

- health or medical outcomes
- disease claims
- supplement, protocol, or treatment claims
- income, earnings, ROI, revenue, or profit claims
- financial advice or investment implications
- legal claims
- credential, certification, licensing, or authority claims
- named competitor accusations
- testimonials without permission or context
- guarantees or timelines that may not be universal

Risk flags do not mean the line is unusable. They mean the client should verify, approve, soften, add context, or route to review before publication.

## Traceability Standard

Every mined asset should carry a location marker:

```text
[00:14:22] "Exact line from transcript"
```

or, when timestamps are unavailable:

```text
Source: transcript section "Why the old method failed," paragraph 8
```

If a line is paraphrased, say so:

```text
Paraphrase from [00:21:05]: [cleaned line]
```

Traceability is what lets future writers use the package confidently.

## Quality Standard

A finished repurposing package should let another writer create emails, pages, clips, and posts without rereading the whole transcript, while still allowing them to verify every claim. It should feel like the speaker's own worldview organized for marketing, not generic content pasted onto the speaker.
