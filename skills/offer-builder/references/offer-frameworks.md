# Offer Frameworks Reference

This reference summarizes the offer-building frameworks used by the `offer-builder` skill in original wording. It is a field guide, not a source of client-specific facts. Use it to think clearly, then verify all claims, proof, prices, guarantees, and scarcity against the client's real business.

## Value Equation

Perceived value rises when the buyer wants the outcome more, believes achievement is more likely, gets progress sooner, and faces less effort or sacrifice.

```text
Perceived value = (dream outcome x perceived likelihood of achievement) / (time delay x effort and sacrifice)
```

Use the four levers like this:

- **Dream outcome:** clarify what the buyer truly wants. Make it specific, emotionally resonant, and expressed in their language.
- **Perceived likelihood:** make success feel believable through proof, process, credentials, fit criteria, diagnostics, milestones, examples, and support.
- **Time delay:** reduce the wait for meaningful progress through quick wins, shorter setup, clear sequencing, and faster feedback.
- **Effort and sacrifice:** remove friction, confusion, complexity, risk, social discomfort, hidden work, and tradeoffs where the business can honestly do so.

Do not fake any lever. If the result takes time or effort, say so and show how the offer makes the path clearer or more supported.

## Grand Slam Offer Construction

A high-value offer is built from the buyer's desired result and obstacles, not from a random list of features.

1. **Name the dream result.** Define the before and after the avatar wants most.
2. **List obstacles.** Capture every barrier that keeps them from the result: knowledge, tools, support, confidence, money, time, accountability, risk, beliefs, and past failures.
3. **Turn obstacles into solutions.** Convert each barrier into something the offer can provide: lesson, service, template, audit, coaching, setup, tool, community, milestone, or guarantee.
4. **Package the solutions.** Decide what belongs in the core offer, what becomes a bonus, what is excluded, and how the buyer experiences the sequence.
5. **Frame the decision.** Name the offer, explain the mechanism, reverse appropriate risk, anchor value, answer objections, and give a truthful reason to act.

The best offers feel tailored because each component exists for a reason.

## Offer Stack and Bonuses

An offer stack makes value visible. It shows the buyer what they receive and why each item matters.

For each stack item, define:

- what it is
- what problem it solves
- how it is delivered
- when the buyer receives it
- why it increases speed, certainty, or ease
- what value anchor is appropriate

Bonuses should handle objections or accelerate implementation. Common jobs for bonuses:

- reduce setup time
- make the first step easier
- provide templates or scripts
- add expert review
- give examples or case studies
- increase accountability
- reduce perceived risk
- help the buyer make a hard decision

Avoid fake bonus economics. A value anchor can be replacement cost, time saved, expert access, comparable service value, risk reduction, or estimated business impact. If the number is not a real price, do not present it as one.

## Guarantee Taxonomy

Risk reversal should fit the business model and be operationally possible.

- **Unconditional guarantee:** the buyer can request a refund inside a clear window with minimal conditions. Best for simple products, lower delivery cost, or businesses that can absorb refund risk.
- **Conditional guarantee:** the buyer qualifies only after completing required actions or meeting stated criteria. Best when the result depends on buyer participation.
- **Anti-guarantee:** the offer clearly states there is no refund or no result guarantee. Best when delivery is customized, capacity is limited, or the work requires committed participation. It must be paired with clear fit criteria.
- **Implied or performance guarantee:** confidence comes from milestones, service terms, outcome-linked components, progress checkpoints, or transparent delivery standards instead of a simple refund promise.

Always state the window, conditions, exclusions, buyer responsibilities, and claim process. Flag health, income, financial, legal, credential, and regulated claims for review.

## Ethical Scarcity and Urgency

Scarcity means limited quantity. Urgency means limited time. Both must be true.

Ethical scarcity can come from:

- limited seats or client capacity
- calendar availability
- cohort size
- inventory
- application review windows
- team delivery bandwidth
- bonuses that require real labor or scheduling

Ethical urgency can come from:

- enrollment deadlines
- live start dates
- seasonal timing
- price changes that are actually scheduled
- expiring audit, onboarding, or implementation windows
- a strategic reason the buyer benefits from acting before a known event

If there is no real deadline or limit, use cost-of-delay framing instead. Explain what waiting may cost, but do not manufacture pressure.

## MAGIC Naming

MAGIC is a naming checklist, not a rigid formula.

- **M, Magnetic reason why:** the distinctive mechanism, belief, discovery, or method behind the offer.
- **A, Avatar:** who it is for or the identity the buyer wants to step into.
- **G, Goal:** the outcome or transformation.
- **I, Interval:** timeframe, milestone, season, cadence, or constraint, only when true.
- **C, Container:** the form of the offer, such as sprint, system, lab, accelerator, audit, challenge, workshop, protocol, roadmap, or membership.

Generate multiple names, then choose the one that is clearest, most credible, easiest to say, and most aligned with the brand voice.

## Offer Roles

Offers work differently depending on their place in the business model.

- **Attraction offer:** creates the first conversion or relationship. It should be easy to understand, low friction, and directly connected to the next step.
- **Core offer:** delivers the main transformation. It needs the strongest mechanism, proof, stack, risk reversal, and sales argument.
- **Upsell offer:** adds speed, depth, support, scale, access, or implementation after purchase. It should feel like the natural next level.
- **Downsell offer:** gives a simpler, lower-cost, or lower-commitment option when the main offer is not the right fit.
- **Continuity offer:** keeps delivering ongoing value through access, support, maintenance, community, replenishment, coaching, or retention.

Build the offer for its role. A lead magnet should not carry the complexity of a core offer, and a continuity offer should justify why value continues over time.

## Offer Document Minimum

A usable offer document should include:

- business, avatar, offer role, price, and sales motion
- one-sentence offer
- dream outcome and mechanism
- value equation analysis
- obstacles mapped to solutions
- offer stack and bonuses
- guarantee or risk reversal
- scarcity and urgency
- MAGIC name options and recommended name
- price framing
- objections handled
- unknowns and claims needing review

## Premium, Free, and Discount Promotions

From the $100M Money Models Lost Chapters (Section A: Attract). These are three macro strategies for presenting your Grand Slam Offer to a market. The offer stays the same — the *wrapper* changes.

**The principle:** "Get flow. Monetize flow. Then add friction." Start with the lowest-friction wrapper to generate demand, then increase price as the market validates the offer. Free and discount are wrappers around the core premium offer, not different offers.

### Premium Promotion (present the offer at full price)

- **What it is:** present the Grand Slam Offer on its own, at full price, no discount or free component
- **When to use:** when the offer is proven, the avatar is warm or product-aware, and you have enough conviction and proof to charge full price
- **Pros:** highest margin, positions as premium, attracts buyers not bargain-hunters
- **Cons:** slowest in cold markets, requires strong proof and conviction
- **Best for:** Stage 2+ businesses with proven offers, warm traffic, high-ticket offers with sales-assisted motion

### Free Promotion (give something for free to generate leads)

- **What it is:** offer a free version, free trial, free audit, free consultation, or free first step. The free item solves a narrow problem; the solution reveals the next problem your core offer solves
- **When to use:** entering new markets, cold audiences, when you lack testimonials, when the offer is unproven
- **The "get flow" logic:** free generates maximum demand and lead flow. You monetize the flow with the core offer after trust is built
- **Hormozi's framing:** "Those who give the most, get the most." He started Gym Launch by flying to gyms for free, doing the work, and only charging for the supplements and memberships. The free service built trust that funded the paid offer
- **Risk:** attracts free-seekers who never convert. Mitigate with qualification and a clear path to the paid offer
- **Best for:** Stage 0-1 businesses, new market entry, lead generation, any business that needs testimonials

### Discount Promotion (offer the core offer at a reduced price)

- **What it is:** present the Grand Slam Offer at a significant discount from an anchored full price. The discount is the reason to act now
- **When to use:** when the offer is proven but needs a push, when entering a market with price-sensitive buyers, when you have a deadline or event (challenge, cohort, launch)
- **Hormozi's framing:** discount is a *promotional wrapper*, not a permanent price cut. The offer must still be profitable at the discount price. After the promotion ends, the price returns to full
- **Risk:** trains buyers to wait for discounts, devalues the offer if used too frequently
- **Best for:** launches, cohorts, time-bound promotions, converting warm leads who are price-sensitive

### The Start-Free-Graduate-to-Premium Sequencing

Hormozi's default playbook for entering new markets:

1. **Start free or massively discounted.** Get the first customers, gather testimonials, build conviction, refine delivery. You don't know what you're doing yet — free gives you wiggle room and forgiveness
2. **Add friction.** Once the offer is proven and testimonials exist, start charging. Raise prices every 5-10 customers (the "raise prices" escalation rule)
3. **Graduate to premium.** Once demand exceeds capacity, present the offer at full premium price. The free/discount era built the proof and demand that justifies the premium

**Key insight:** the progression is unidirectional. Going from premium back to free signals desperation. Start low, build proof, climb.

## Lead Magnet Design

From $100M Leads. A lead magnet is the smallest unit of free value that makes a prospect *show interest*. It solves one narrow problem so well that solving it reveals the next problem — which your core offer solves for money.

### The Problem-Solution Cycle

Every problem has a solution. Every solution reveals more problems. Smaller problem-solution cycles sit inside larger ones. Choose a problem narrow enough to solve completely for free, but specific enough that solving it makes the next problem obvious.

**Example (Hormozi's book as a lead magnet):**
- The book solves the narrow problem: "How do I get more leads?"
- Solving it reveals the next problem: "Now I have leads, but I need to scale the business"
- The next problem is solved by the core offer: Acquisition.com invests in the business to help it scale

### 3 Lead Magnet Types

1. **Reveal their problem (diagnostics/assessments):** the prospect doesn't know they have the problem. The magnet is a quiz, assessment, audit, or calculator that reveals the gap. Best for unaware avatars who need to see the problem before they want the solution

2. **Samples and trials:** the prospect knows the problem but isn't sure your solution works. The magnet is a free sample, a trial, a mini-version, a "fun-size" portion. Best for solution-aware avatars who need proof

3. **One step of a multi-step process:** the prospect wants the full result but is overwhelmed. The magnet solves one step completely (the easiest or fastest one). Best for avatars who know what they want but need momentum. Each step solved reveals the next step

### 7-Step Lead Magnet Construction

1. **Define the problem** you will solve and for whom. Pick a narrow, specific problem from the problem-solution cycle. Ensure your core offer solves the *next* problem
2. **Figure out how to solve it.** The solution must genuinely work, not be a teaser
3. **Figure out how to deliver it.** PDF, video, tool, template, calculator, checklist, mini-course. Delivery must be instant and frictionless
4. **Test names.** Try 3-5 names. Choose the one that is clearest and most compelling. The name is the hook
5. **Make consumption easy.** The magnet should be consumable in under 10 minutes. If it's longer, it won't be consumed and won't build trust
6. **Make it genuinely excellent.** A mediocre free thing damages trust. The magnet should make prospects feel "stupid saying no" to more
7. **Build in the "I want more" mechanism.** The magnet must naturally lead to the next step. The pretzel analogy: the salty pretzel creates thirst for the drink. The lead magnet creates desire for the core offer

## Money Model Offer Library

From $100M Money Models (How to Make Money). These are 15 named, scripted offer patterns organized by their role in the revenue sequence. Each is a *promotional wrapper* around the Grand Slam Offer, designed to maximize a specific outcome.

### Attraction Offers (turn strangers into customers)

- **Win Your Money Back:** pay now, get money back if you hit a goal (by results, by actions, or both). ~10% claim rate makes it profitable. Store-credit-over-time variant keeps revenue in-house
- **Giveaways:** grand prize + "partial scholarship" promotional offer to all non-winners. The eligibility survey doubles as lead qualification. 7-day urgency windows
- **Decoy Offer:** advertise a cheap thing, present the premium offer side-by-side when they engage. "Are you here for free stuff or lasting results?" permission question filters buyers from seekers
- **Buy X Get Y Free:** reframe pricing perception. "Buy 1 Get 2 Free" outperforms "66% off" because the buyer perceives gaining rather than losing. Give more free than paid items
- **Pay Less Now or Pay More Later:** delayed payment + conditional guarantee, or pay-now discount. Creates urgency through price escalation over time

### Upsell Offers (extract more cash per customer)

- **Classic Upsell:** "You can't have X without Y." Solve the next revealed problem after the initial purchase. Natural, not forced
- **Menu Upsell:** 4 tactics — Unsell (make them want it more by restricting it), Prescribe (recommend one specific option), A/B (present two and let them choose), Card-on-File (smooth the upgrade path). The Economist decoy pricing play applies
- **Anchor Upsell:** present a 5-10x premium offer first, get "the gasp," then rescue with the main offer which now feels reasonable
- **Rollover Upsell:** credit the prior purchase toward a more expensive next offer. Winback campaigns use this effectively (20% response rate in Hormozi's data)

### Downsell Offers (turn a no into a yes)

- **Payment Plan Downsell:** 7-step escalation — full-pay reward → financing → half-now → want-check → thirds → equal payments → free trial. The "seesaw" variant alternates between payment and value reduction
- **Trial With Penalty:** free if you meet terms, pay if you don't. Reuses the Win-Your-Money-Back criteria as a payment trigger
- **Feature Downsell:** lower the price by removing features, not by discounting. Removing the guarantee often triggers a re-upsell (buyer prefers the safety)

### Continuity Offers (maximize LTV)

- **Continuity Bonus:** add a recurring component as a bonus to the initial purchase
- **Continuity Discount:** offer a lower recurring rate for committing upfront
- **Waived Fee:** waive the setup or initiation fee in exchange for a longer commitment

### Money Model Construction Principles

- **Stage-gated implementation:** perfect one offer, automate it, then add the next. Never build the whole model at once
- **Simple scales, fancy fails:** 100 ways to offer one product beats 100 products offered one way
- **Price-raising protocol:** start low for yeses and feedback, raise until revenue peaks, then hold
- **Affiliate gap-filling:** use others' products to fill gaps in the Money Model with zero delivery overhead
