# Email Patterns Reference

This reference is the field guide for the `email-writer` skill. It contains the copywriting patterns extracted from a real-world corpus of high-performing marketing emails, plus the principles behind why the patterns work. Use it to think clearly, then pull real stories, proof, offers, and voice from the client wiki and verify every claim against the client's real business.

The patterns below are observed structures, not rigid templates. Adapt them to the brand voice, the avatar, and the offer. Never copy an example verbatim unless it is the client's own material.

---

## Corpus Summary

The patterns in this reference are drawn from 23 real emails across three senders:

| Sender | Volume | Avg words | Avg words/sentence | % short sentences (≤8 words) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alex Hormozi (Acquisition.com) | 17 | 480 | 13.1 | 32% |
| Leila Hormozi | 2 | 1,076 | 18.5 | 17% |
| Alex Becker (HYROS) | 3 | 292 | 9.6 | 52% |

The Hormozi style is the dominant model: medium-length value emails, mixed sentence rhythm, story-driven, soft CTA. The Becker style is shorter and more direct, used for promotional urgency. The Leila style is longer and leadership-tone, used for culture and retention content.

## Sender Name and Addressing

### How the recipient is addressed

**None of the senders use the recipient's first name.** There are no personalization tokens, no "Hi [Name]," no "Hey Matt." The open is earned through the subject line and the first sentence, not through fake familiarity.

| Sender | Greeting style | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Alex Hormozi | No greeting. Opens straight into the content. | `Most businesses fail because they work on the wrong stuff.` |
| Leila Hormozi | Collective address. | `Team, I heard something yesterday…` |
| Alex Becker | Generic, never named. | `Hey. This is Alex Becker. CEO of HYROS.` |

**Rule:** Do not use personalization tokens. Do not address the recipient by name unless the brand voice explicitly calls for it. Earn the open with the subject and the first line.

### Sign-off conventions

| Sender | Sign-off | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Alex Hormozi | `- Alex` | First name, casual. No title. |
| Leila Hormozi | No personal sign-off | Internal memo format. The footer handles identity. |
| Alex Becker | `-Alex Becker (CEO of HYROS)` | Name + title. More formal, used for promo trust. |

**Rule:** Default to a casual first-name sign-off (`- Alex`). Follow the brand voice guide if it specifies otherwise.

## Subject Line Patterns

Five recurring structures. Average length: 4 to 7 words. Sentence case or Title Case. No emoji. No ALL CAPS (except brand names).

### 1. Numbered list

Frames the email as a quick, structured read.

- `3 ways to get ignored`
- `4 Step Process To Set Goals`
- `4 Things That Hold Business Owners Back`
- `4 Winning Strategies`

### 2. Question

Invites the reader to self-diagnose.

- `Are you the bottleneck?`
- `What Business Are You REALLY in?`
- `What is "the swamp?"`

### 3. Contrarian statement

Creates tension with a common belief.

- `Say no. Win bigger.`
- `You can't do everything anymore`
- `Worth Doing Badly`

### 4. Curiosity or intrigue

Withholds the answer to pull the open.

- `The #1 thing`
- `Private video From Leila`
- `My AAA Close Framework`

### 5. Series prefix

A brandable recurring column. Builds recognition over time.

- `Mozi Minute: AI didn't rewrite the rules of business`
- `Mozi Minute: Always have a group`
- `Mozi Minute: The second steak problem`
- `Mozi Minute: What to do if you need more leads`

### 6. Direct promo (sparing)

Used only for real events, deadlines, or offers.

- `Since you wanted to scale your business`
- `My first ever Offers Workshop`
- `24 Hours Left To Lock In HYROS grandfather pricing` (Becker — urgency + deadline in subject)

### Becker urgency variants

Parenthetical deadline in the subject. Used only for real, time-boxed promotions.

- `I give up. I'm giving HYROS away for free. (Only today)`
- `Last Time We Do This. Hyros is free now. (But this ends today)`

## Preview Text

**There is no separate preheader text.** The first sentence of the body is the preview. It is written to function in the inbox preview pane and as the email's opening line. One sentence, one line, designed to pull the reader in.

### Examples (first sentence = preview)

- `Most businesses fail because they work on the wrong stuff.`
- `Everyone is obsessed with AI.`
- `Quick one.`
- `I hate most business goal-setting advice out there.`
- `So, you want to scale your business? Cool.`
- `There's a revenue zone we call "the swamp."`

**Rule:** Do not write a separate preheader. Do not let "View in browser" or footer text eat the preview. The first sentence is the preview.

## Opening Structure

The Hormozi opener follows a consistent three-beat pattern. No pleasantries, no "I hope you're well." Straight into the content.

### The three-beat opener

1. **Bold claim or observation** (1 sentence)
2. **Bridge or reframe** (1 sentence)
3. **Transition** — `Here's why:`, `Here's the 4-step…`, `So I'm going to give you…`

### Examples

**The #1 thing:**
> `Most businesses fail because they work on the wrong stuff.` → `Not because they don't work hard.` → `This is mostly because the hardest problems are ones that you usually don't know how to solve yet…`

**4 Winning Strategies:**
> `Most businesses try to be marginally better at everything and end up dominant at nothing.` → `There are only four competitive advantages that actually matter.` → `Pick one and you'll crush 99% of your competition.`

**3 ways to get ignored:**
> `Every day we try to influence people in our business to do things we want them to do.` → `We try to influence them with money, praise, the company vision, etc.` → `But some people still don't do what you want them to do.`

**The second steak problem:**
> `One of our portfolio companies had an ascension/renewal issue despite having absurdly high NPS…` → `So we dug into the customer calls, did surveys etc, and everything kept coming back the same: people loved the service.` → `So, why weren't people renewing/buying more?`

## Content Types

Six templates. Each drives the structure of the body. Choose one before writing.

### 1. Contrarian Lesson

**Structure:** "Most people do X. Here's why Y wins."

Opens with a common belief or behavior, reframes it, delivers the lesson, closes with the application.

**Example — "The #1 thing":**
- Open: `Most businesses fail because they work on the wrong stuff.`
- Reframe: `Not because they don't work hard.`
- Lesson: Working on second-best problems gives second-best returns. The real value is in the #1 constraint.
- Application: `Ask yourself: "If we could only work on ONE thing for the next 90 days that 2-3x'd the business, what would it be?"`

**Example — "4 Winning Strategies":**
- Open: `Most businesses try to be marginally better at everything and end up dominant at nothing.`
- Lesson: There are only four competitive advantages that matter. Pick one.

### 2. Numbered Framework

**Structure:** "Here are N ways/steps/things…"

Opens with the premise, lists the items with one-line explanations, closes with the reframe or application.

**Example — "3 ways to get ignored":**
- Open: `Every day we try to influence people in our business to do things we want them to do.`
- Premise: `So I'm going to give you three ways to guarantee people DON'T do what you want them to do.`
- Items:
  - `Don't tell them` — no expectations, no metrics, no check-ins.
  - `Make sure they don't know how` — no training, no priorities.
  - `Don't give motivation` — no pay, no praise, no purpose.
- Application: `Now flip it. Do they know what you want? Do they know how? Do they want to?`

### 3. Story or Metaphor

**Structure:** A real moment or portfolio-company story, then the principle it illustrates. The metaphor does the teaching.

**Example — "The second steak problem":**
- Story: A portfolio company had high NPS but low renewals. They dug in and found people loved the service but didn't need more — they needed different.
- Metaphor: The fancy restaurant. You order the best steak. The waiter asks if you'd like a second steak. You say no. Not because the steak was bad, but because you're full. You might want dessert — a different solution to a different problem.
- Principle: `Sometimes the customer doesn't need more — they need something different. And usually the "different" thing is the solution to the problem your first solution created.`

### 4. Case Study or Proof

**Structure:** A named person's result, then the lesson behind it. Specific and verifiable.

**Example — "Are you the bottleneck?":**
- Open: `After coming to the workshop… Rhys was able to buy back his time and focus on higher leverage work.`
- Lesson: The founder was the bottleneck. The workshop helped him delegate and focus on high-leverage work.
- CTA: `Discover if you're a fit for our workshops here.`

### 5. Direct Promo

**Structure:** "You want X? Here's how we help."

Short, direct, qualification-framed CTA. Used sparingly — only for real events, deadlines, or offers.

**Example — "Since you wanted to scale your business":**
- Open: `So, you want to scale your business? Cool.`
- Body: `Here's how we can help: We're getting together at our Las Vegas Headquarters. And we're showing a handful of entrepreneurs and business owners the growth methods we (personally) use to scale companies.`
- CTA: `Tap here to see if you qualify.`
- Scarcity (real): `FYI: We're only accepting ~100 business owners per workshop since it's physically at our HQ in-person.`

**Example — Becker promo:**
- Open: `Hey. This is Alex Becker. CEO of HYROS. Let's cut the fluff.`
- Body: `I'd like to let you try HYROS for free. BUT before you consider, I want you to understand one thing. HYROS is the only ad tracking system that GUARANTEES an increase in AD ROI.`
- Proof: `This is why brands like Tony Robbins, Four Seasons, Playboy, Alex Hormozi… use our tracking & ai optimization.`
- CTA: `You can just go here and try HYROS free (24 hours only).`

### 6. Internal Memo

**Structure:** A longer, leadership-tone narrative addressed to "Team." Used for culture, belief, and retention content.

**Example — Leila Hormozi, "Worth Doing Badly":**
- Open: `Team, I heard something yesterday that I have not been able to stop thinking about and absolutely LOVED.`
- Core idea: `Good things are worth doing badly.`
- Body: Three scenarios — a leader avoiding a hard conversation, a person stuck on slide four of a deck, someone sitting on an idea for four months.
- Personal story: Leila avoided making content for four years, redoing cover art 100x, protecting herself from a bad first video.
- Lesson: `Good things are worth doing badly.` Start before you're ready.

## Body Structure and Sentence Rhythm

### Paragraph length

1 to 2 sentences, then a line break. Lots of white space. The email should be scannable on a phone without zooming.

### Sentence length

Mix very short punchy lines with medium explanation sentences.

- Average: 10 to 14 words per sentence (Hormozi).
- Roughly 30 to 45 percent of sentences are 8 words or fewer.
- Shortest lines are often the most powerful: `Now flip it.` / `Not because they don't work hard.` / `Interesting.` / `Quick one.`

### Section headers

Bold headers break the email into scannable blocks. They are short, label-like, and promise the next section.

- `Interesting Case Study:`
- `Why Second-Best Problems Are Deadly:`
- `Example:`
- `How To Find Your #1 Thing:`
- `Common #1 constraints by stage:`

### Conversational asides

Personality, not filler. Used to break rhythm and add voice.

- `Food for thought (pun intended).`
- `Best way to set someone up to fail.`
- `And then you have an awkward moment.`

### Questions to the reader

Pull the reader into the reasoning. Often used right before the lesson or the CTA.

- `Ask yourself: "If we could only work on ONE thing for the next 90 days that 2-3x'd the business, what would it be?"`
- `So, why weren't people renewing/buying more?`
- `"Am I trying to sell a second steak?"`

### Directive transitions

Move the reader from story to lesson to action.

- `Now flip it.`
- `So if you aren't getting as many upsells as you want…`
- `If you do all three of these things, you will virtually guarantee nobody does what you want.`

## Call to Action Patterns

### Hormozi: soft, qualification-framed, single-line, at the very end

The frame is always "see if you qualify / see if it's a fit." Puts the reader in control, creates qualification tension.

- `See if you qualify here.`
- `Tap here to see if it's a fit.`
- `Discover if you're a fit for our workshops here.`
- `See if you are a fit here.`
- `Tap here to come to our next scaling workshop.`

### Hormozi: P.S. pattern (Mozi Minute)

Used for ongoing-value emails. A teaser line + link after the sign-off.

- `PS - happy tuesday ([LINK])`
- `PS - Life with a group... ([LINK])`
- `PS - When you ask for the sale at the wrong time… ([LINK])`
- `PS - Remember... ([LINK])`

### Hormozi: content CTA

For emails that drive to a video or resource rather than a workshop.

- `Watch the video here.`
- `Watch the video by tapping here.`
- `Here's the private video from Leila on leadership.`

### Becker: direct promo CTA

More direct, but still single-line. Used only for real, time-boxed promotions.

- `>> Book Your Call`
- `You can just go here and try HYROS free (24 hours only).`
- `After you sign up. Run HYROS. Get results. THEN pay after you get results.`

### What to avoid

Never use:

- `BUY NOW`
- `CLICK HERE TO PURCHASE`
- Multi-line CTA blocks
- Button images as the only CTA (the text CTA should work on its own)
- More than one CTA per email

## Email Length by Type

| Email type | Target word count | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Broadcast / value | 200 to 600 | One idea, one lesson, one soft CTA. |
| Welcome | 150 to 400 | Set expectations, shift one belief. |
| Ongoing value (Mozi Minute) | 350 to 500 | Story or observation + lesson + P.S. CTA. |
| Onboarding | 100 to 250 | Drive one activation action. Short and directive. |
| Retention / win-back | 150 to 300 | Acknowledge, re-engage, point to next milestone. |
| Direct promo | 150 to 300 | Short, direct, deadline-stated. |
| Internal memo | 600 to 1,200 | Longer leadership narrative. Used sparingly. |

**Rule:** Value per second, not seconds of value. If the email is long, every paragraph must earn its place. Overwhelm is the number-one reason readers disengage.

## Underlying Principles

These principles come from the Hormozi book corpus and explain why the patterns above work.

### Value per second, not seconds of value

> "Think value per second, not seconds of value. Less but better is better than more and decent. Overwhelm is the #1 reason for churn."

Every word earns its place. A short, dense email beats a long, padded one. This is the governing principle behind the short paragraphs, the one-idea-per-email rule, and the length targets.

### Provide ongoing value to get ongoing customers

> "I have a friend who ran a newsletter business that made $500k per month. I asked him what he did to decrease churn. He said 'I can tell you all the things that didn't work. When churn was the lowest I did one Q&A call per month and I just stayed on until everyone's questions were answered. Then I wrote one long physical newsletter per month.' … everything I added increased churn."

More is not better. Better is better. The cadence that keeps churn lowest is often less frequent and higher quality. When planning an ongoing cadence, resist the urge to add more touchpoints. Make each one count.

### Activation points drive retention

> "Every customer that does (X thing) or gets (Y result) stays for longer than customers who don't."

For onboarding emails, the goal is not to teach everything — it is to drive the one action that predicts retention. Identify the activation event and point every onboarding email at it.

### The headline principle

> "Double your reading speed in 3 hours, or it's free."

The strongest hooks combine an outcome, a timeframe, and a risk reversal. Apply this to subject lines and first sentences: state the outcome, make it specific, and remove the reader's risk of engaging.

### Give a good reason

> "Going out of business, all products must go in 30 days is a very good reason to have 90% off all products. If you just said 90% off all products, you likely wouldn't get the same response."

When asking for the sale or the click, give a real reason. A discount without a reason feels manipulative. A discount with a true reason feels honest. This applies to promotional emails: state the real reason for the offer, the deadline, or the scarcity.
