# Voice and Clarity Frameworks

> Original paraphrased summaries of common clarity and messaging frameworks. No book text is reproduced here. Use these as the reference behind the brand-voice-extractor skill.

These frameworks answer two questions: what does the brand say (message clarity) and how does it say it (voice). Clarity comes first. A distinctive voice built on a confusing message just makes the confusion memorable.

---

## 1. The Seven-Part Clarity Framework (SB7)

A story-shaped way to organize a brand message so a customer instantly understands their role and the next step. The customer is the hero, not the brand. The brand is the guide.

1. **A character (the hero).** Identify the customer and the one thing they want that relates to what the brand sells. Keep the want simple and survival-relevant (save money, save time, gain status, build relationships, find meaning).
2. **Has a problem.** Name the problem on three levels:
   - **External:** the tangible, surface problem (my lawn is patchy).
   - **Internal:** how the external problem makes them feel (I feel embarrassed and like a failure at home upkeep).
   - **Philosophical:** why it is just plain wrong that they have this problem (you deserve to be proud of where you live).
   Most brands only talk about the external problem. The internal problem is usually the real reason people buy. Also name the villain: personify the root cause of the problem as a single antagonist the message can push against.
3. **And meets a guide.** The brand positions itself as the guide, not the hero. A guide earns the role with two things:
   - **Empathy:** show you understand and care about the customer's pain (I know how frustrating this is).
   - **Authority:** show you have the competence to help (proof, results, experience, credentials, testimonials).
4. **Who gives them a plan.** Reduce the customer's perceived risk and confusion with a clear plan:
   - **Process plan:** the simple steps to buy or to get the result (usually three to four steps).
   - **Agreement plan:** the promises or values that remove fear and objection.
5. **And calls them to action.** Make the next step obvious. Use two kinds of calls to action:
   - **Direct CTA:** buy now, book a call, schedule, sign up. Ask for the sale plainly.
   - **Transitional CTA:** a lower-commitment step for people not ready to buy (free guide, sample, webinar) that keeps the relationship going.
6. **That helps them avoid failure.** Briefly show the stakes: what does the customer stand to lose if they do nothing. Use sparingly; a little fear is motivating, too much repels.
7. **And ends in success.** Paint a clear, specific picture of the after state. Show how life looks once the problem is solved. People need to see where the brand is taking them.

Discipline: run every asset (site, email, page, talk) through these seven beats. If a beat is missing or muddy, the customer feels confused and confused customers do not act.

## 2. The One-Liner

A single, repeatable sentence that says what the brand does and why it matters. Used in introductions, bios, taglines, email signatures, and the top of pages. Two compatible builds:

### Short build: Problem + Solution + Result
- **Problem:** the pain the customer feels.
- **Solution:** what the brand offers to solve it.
- **Result:** the happy outcome once it is solved.

Example shape: "Most homeowners are embarrassed by a patchy lawn. We deliver a simple seasonal care plan so your yard becomes the best-looking one on the block."

### Four-part build: Character + Problem + Plan + Success
- **Character:** name the customer.
- **Problem:** the problem they face.
- **Plan:** the simple path the brand provides.
- **Success:** the successful end state.

Rules for both builds:
- Lead with the customer's problem, not the company history.
- Keep it conversational enough to say out loud without stumbling.
- Cut jargon and internal language. Test it on someone outside the business.
- One clear result beats three vague benefits.

## 3. Voice Extraction Checklist

Use this to capture how a specific person or brand actually sounds, pulled from real source material (transcripts, emails, posts, calls, existing pages). Record concrete examples, not adjectives.

- **Common phrases:** words, expressions, and sentence openers the brand uses repeatedly. Collect real examples verbatim.
- **Taboo phrases:** words the brand would never use, corporate cliches to avoid, terms that feel off-brand or that the audience distrusts.
- **Tone:** the emotional register (warm, blunt, playful, authoritative, irreverent, calm). Note how it shifts by context if it does.
- **Rhythm:** sentence length and cadence. Short and punchy, long and flowing, or a mix. Note use of fragments, one-line paragraphs, questions, and lists.
- **Level of polish:** raw and conversational versus refined and formal. Contractions, slang, and profanity in or out. Grammar strictness.
- **Enemy language:** how the brand names and talks about the villain or the "them" it stands against. The us-versus-them framing.
- **Proof language:** how the brand backs claims (numbers, stories, testimonials, demonstrations, credentials) and how it phrases proof.
- **CTA style:** how the brand asks people to act. Soft or direct, imperative or invitational, the exact call-to-action wording it favors.

Output of the checklist is a set of do and do-not rules with real sample lines, not a vibe.

## 4. Plain-Language Writing Rules

Rules that make copy easy to read and act on. Apply these to any brand-voice output and pass finished copy to the human-editor skill for a final tightening.

- **Simple reading level:** write so a busy, distracted reader understands it on the first pass. Aim low on reading grade, not to dumb down but to remove friction.
- **Active voice:** prefer "we mow your lawn" over "your lawn is mowed by us." The subject does the action.
- **Present tense:** default to present tense for immediacy and clarity; switch tense only when meaning requires it.
- **Short sentences:** one idea per sentence. Break long sentences into two. Short sentences carry the important lines.
- **Remove redundant words:** cut words that repeat an idea already stated ("free gift," "advance planning," "end result"). Every word earns its place.
- **Avoid unnecessary adverbs:** replace "runs very quickly" with a stronger verb ("sprints"). Cut "really," "very," "quite," "just" when they add nothing.
- **Positive language:** state what to do, not only what to avoid. Positive framing is clearer and more motivating than negative framing.

Voice and clarity are not opposites. The voice makes the message distinctive; the plain-language rules make it land. Keep both.
