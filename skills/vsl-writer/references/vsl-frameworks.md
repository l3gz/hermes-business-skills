# VSL Frameworks Reference

This reference summarizes the VSL frameworks used by the `vsl-writer` skill. It is a field guide distilled from the indexed sources (VSL Script Writer v2 notebook, 47 sources covering copywriting, conversion psychology, SaaS, ecommerce, and risk reversal). Use it to structure the script, then verify all claims, proof, prices, and guarantees against the client's real offer.

## VSL Anatomy — Section-by-Section

A high-converting VSL follows a tested anatomy. Each section has a job, a time budget, and a word count range. Skip a section only with a reason.

### 1. The Hook (0:00-0:15)

The hook's only job is to earn the next 15 seconds. It must create a curiosity gap, a pattern interrupt, or a desire promise strong enough that the viewer commits to watching.

**Hook patterns (choose by awareness level):**

- **Curiosity hook (cold traffic):** "In the next X minutes, I'll show you [specific result] — even if [common objection]." No product name, no jargon. The gap between what they know and what you promise keeps them watching.
- **Problem hook (cold traffic):** "If you've ever [specific pain], then what I'm about to show you will [specific relief]." Leads with the avatar's exact pain in their own words.
- **Story hook (warm traffic):** "X years ago, I was [relatable low point]. Today, [contrast]. Here's what changed — and why it matters for you."
- **Contrarian hook (warm traffic):** "Everything you've been told about [topic] is wrong. Here's the truth." Pushes against a false belief the avatar already suspects is broken.
- **Proof hook (hot traffic):** "[Specific result] in [specific timeframe]. Here's exactly how." Leads with the outcome the buyer already wants.
- **Direct offer hook (hot traffic):** "If you want [result], here's the fastest way I've found to get it." Skips curiosity, goes straight to value.

The hook must pay off within the intro. Empty curiosity ("You won't believe what happened next") erodes trust and spikes drop-off.

### 2. The Intro / Big Promise (0:15-1:00)

State who this is for, what they will get from watching, and why it matters now. Set the stakes. This section earns the viewer's commitment to the full video.

Structure:
- **Who this is for:** call out the avatar specifically ("If you're a [role] struggling with [problem]...")
- **What you'll learn or get:** the big promise in specific terms
- **Why now:** the urgency or relevance that makes watching today matter
- **The commitment ask:** "Give me X minutes and I'll show you Y"

### 3. Origin Story / Credibility (1:00-3:00)

Establish why the viewer should listen. This is not a biography — it is a credibility asset that builds the attractive character's authority and foreshadows the mechanism.

Structure:
- The before state (relatable struggle)
- The discovery or turning point (the moment the mechanism was found)
- The after state (the result that proves it works)
- The bridge to the viewer ("and that's what I want to share with you")

Keep it under 3 minutes. The origin story exists to earn the mechanism section, not to center the founder.

### 4. Problem Amplification (3:00-6:00)

Name the real problem in the avatar's language. Agitate the pain. Introduce the villain — the false belief, broken system, or external enemy the brand stands against.

Structure:
- The surface problem (what they say out loud)
- The real problem (the deeper diagnosis)
- The cost of not solving it (emotional, financial, identity)
- The villain (what or who is really to blame — false belief, bad advice, broken system)
- The false promise (what they've tried that failed and why)

### 5. The Mechanism / Unique Solution (6:00-10:00)

Reveal the unique mechanism that makes the result achievable. This is the "why this works when other things failed" section — the most important section for differentiation.

Structure:
- The big idea (the new approach, the paradigm shift)
- The mechanism explained (how it works, step by step)
- Why it works when alternatives failed (contrast with the villain/false belief)
- The proof it works (a single strongest example, not a testimonial dump yet)

### 6. Transformation / Proof (10:00-15:00)

Show the before-and-after. Real testimonials, case studies, demonstrations. Make the result believable for this specific avatar.

Structure:
- 2-4 testimonials matched to the avatar's objections (use diverse proof — different backgrounds, starting points, and outcomes)
- A demonstration if the product can be shown working
- The common objection proof ("even if you've never..." or "even if you've tried...")
- The emotional transformation, not just the practical result

Flag any testimonial or result that is not from real source material.

### 7. Offer Reveal (15:00-20:00)

Introduce the offer as the vehicle for the transformation. Frame it as the logical next step the viewer has been waiting for, not a pitch.

Structure:
- The transition ("so I put together something for people who want to do this")
- The offer name and one-sentence description
- The dream outcome the offer delivers
- The mechanism delivered through the offer

### 8. Stack Build (20:00-25:00)

Present each deliverable, its value anchor, and the objection it removes. Build the perceived value before revealing price.

**Stack build rules:**
- One item at a time
- Each item: what it is, what problem it solves, how it is delivered, value anchor
- Value anchors must be honest (replacement cost, time saved, expert access, comparable service value)
- Do not invent retail prices — label subjective anchors as estimates
- Build to a total stack value before the price reveal

### 9. Price Reveal and Anchor (25:00-28:00)

Reveal the price against the stacked value. This is where anchoring psychology matters most.

**Price anchoring principles:**
- **Relative, not absolute:** $997 feels expensive alone but cheap next to a $9,700 stack. Always reveal price against the total stack value.
- **The $1-for-$X return logic:** frame the price as a fraction of the value or the result ("for every $1 you invest, you get $X in value back")
- **Cost of alternatives:** anchor against what the buyer would pay for the components separately, or for an alternative solution (coaching, agency, done-for-you)
- **Cost of inaction:** frame the price against the cost of staying stuck (time lost, money burned on failed attempts, emotional toll)
- **Decoy pricing:** offer three tiers where the middle is the obvious choice (only if the business genuinely has three tiers)

Never reveal price before the stack is built. The stack is what makes the price feel logical.

### 10. Risk Reversal (28:00-30:00)

Present the guarantee. Make acting feel safer than not acting.

**Guarantee types for VSLs:**

- **Unconditional:** "Try it for X days. If it's not for you, email me and I'll refund every cent." Lowest friction, highest risk for the business.
- **Conditional:** "Complete the program. If you do the work and don't get [specific result], I'll refund you." Requires buyer action, protects against non-participation.
- **Action-based:** "Do the steps. If you hit the milestones and it doesn't work, I'll work with you 1-on-1 until it does." Highest perceived value.
- **Trial offers:** "Start for $1 today, pay the rest only if it works." Reduces entry friction dramatically.
- **Implied/performance:** confidence comes from milestones, service terms, or outcome-linked components rather than a simple refund.

Present the guarantee on camera with clear terms, conditions, and the claim process.

### 11. Scarcity and Urgency (30:00-32:00)

Real reasons to act now. Never fake these.

**Ethical scarcity sources:**
- Limited seats or client capacity
- Cohort start dates
- Bonus availability tied to real limits
- Application or enrollment windows

**Ethical urgency sources:**
- Enrollment deadlines
- Live start dates
- Price changes that are actually scheduled
- Seasonal timing or strategic windows

If there is no real deadline, use cost-of-delay framing instead. Never fabricate a countdown.

### 12. The CTA (32:00-35:00)

Direct, specific, repeated. Tell the viewer exactly what to do, what happens next, and why doing it now is the right move.

**CTA structure:**
1. The action verb ("Click the button below")
2. The destination ("to claim your spot" / "to start your trial")
3. The immediate next step ("you'll be taken to a secure checkout")
4. The risk reducer ("covered by the X-day guarantee")
5. The reason to act now ("bonuses expire at midnight")
6. The repetition (state the CTA 2-3 times in the final minutes)

Weak CTAs ("learn more," "click here") kill conversion. Every CTA must answer: what do I do, what happens, and why now.

### 13. Close / FAQ (35:00+)

Handle remaining objections, restate the big promise, final CTA.

Structure:
- 3-5 common questions or objections answered directly
- Restate the big promise and the transformation
- Final CTA with full risk reversal and urgency

## Story Frameworks

### The Hero's Journey (VSL-adapted)

1. **The ordinary world** — the avatar's current state (relatable)
2. **The call to adventure** — the desire for something more
3. **Refusal of the call** — the doubts, false beliefs, past failures
4. **Meeting the mentor** — the attractive character / mechanism
5. **Crossing the threshold** — the decision to try the offer
6. **Tests, allies, enemies** — the obstacles and how the offer helps
7. **The ordeal** — the hardest part of the transformation
8. **The reward** — the result
9. **The road back** — integration into normal life
10. **The resurrection** — the final test that proves the transformation is real
11. **Return with the elixir** — the new normal, the identity shift

Not every VSL needs the full arc. Use the beats that serve the offer.

### Before-After-Bridge (BAB)

1. **Before** — the current pain state
2. **After** — the desired future state
3. **Bridge** — the mechanism that gets them there

Tighter than the hero's journey; best for shorter VSLs and ecommerce.

### The Origin Story (info-product standard)

1. **The struggle** — where the founder started (relatable low point)
2. **The discovery** — the moment the mechanism was found
3. **The validation** — proof it works (first results)
4. **The systematization** — turning it into a repeatable offer
5. **The invitation** — offering it to the viewer

### The Epiphany

1. **The false belief** — what the avatar currently believes
2. **The contradiction** — evidence that challenges the belief
3. **The epiphany** — the realization moment
4. **The new belief** — what is now true
5. **The offer** — the vehicle for the new belief

## Conversion Psychology Principles

- **Loss aversion:** frame the cost of inaction as a loss, not just the price as a cost. People feel losses 2x as intensely as equivalent gains.
- **Social proof sequencing:** start with relatable proof (similar avatar), build to aspirational proof (best result), end with consensus proof (many people like you).
- **Authority building:** establish credentials through origin story, results, and expertise — not by listing acronyms.
- **Scarcity in the close:** real scarcity converts best when stated once, clearly, near the CTA — not repeated throughout.
- **Objection preemption:** answer objections in the script before they arise in the viewer's mind. Reactive objection handling (comments, FAQ at the end) converts worse than preemptive.
- **The Zeigarnik effect:** open loops (unanswered questions, incomplete stories) hold attention. Close them later in the script.
- **Specificity converts:** "lost 23 lbs in 11 weeks" beats "lost weight fast." Specific numbers, timeframes, and mechanisms build credibility.
- **Future pacing:** have the viewer imagine the after state in sensory detail before the offer reveal.

## B2B / SaaS VSL Patterns

SaaS VSLs differ from info-product VSLs. The buyer is rational, the purchase is often committee-based, and the proof is ROI math.

- **Lead with the problem and cost of status quo:** "Your team is losing X hours per week to [problem]. That's $Y per month in wasted payroll."
- **Demo-driven mechanism:** show the product working. Screen recordings, workflow walkthroughs, before/after comparisons.
- **ROI math in the stack:** frame the price against time saved, revenue gained, or cost avoided. "$99/month saves you 20 hours of $50/hr work = $1,000/month in recovered payroll."
- **Trial or demo CTA instead of direct purchase:** "Start a 14-day free trial" or "Book a demo" reduces friction for committee buyers.
- **Shorter origin story:** the B2B buyer cares about the product and the ROI, not the founder's life story. Lead with the buyer's problem.
- **PLG funnel integration:** if the product is product-led (freemium, trial), the VSL should drive the activation event (signup, first action), not the purchase.
- **Enterprise VSL variant:** longer, more technical, with security/compliance/integration proof. CTA is often "talk to sales" not "start trial."

## Ecommerce VSL Patterns

Ecommerce VSLs are tighter, demo-led, and handle price comparison directly.

- **Lead with the product demo:** show the product in use within the first 30 seconds. The product is the star, not the founder.
- **Problem-solution-demo arc:** name the problem, show the solution (the product), demonstrate it working. Tight 3-8 minute structure.
- **UGC-style authenticity:** user-generated content style (phone footage, real customer clips) outperforms studio polish for many ecommerce categories.
- **Price comparison handled honestly:** "You could pay $X for [alternative] that does less. Or $Y for [our product] that does more." Don't hide from commodity competition — differentiate on value.
- **Direct purchase CTA, minimal friction:** "Click to order" with one-click checkout, free shipping, easy returns.
- **Risk reversal dominant:** free returns, money-back guarantees, "try it risk-free" are the primary conversion levers.

## Retention Principles

A long VSL must earn attention every 60-90 seconds. Tactics:

- **Pattern interrupts:** change the visual, the angle, the speaker, or the tone every 60-90 seconds
- **Open loops:** raise a question, promise the answer later
- **Story beats:** a new anecdote, a twist, a revelation
- **Text overlays:** burn in key phrases, stats, and value anchors to reinforce the audio
- **Pacing shifts:** slow for emotion, fast for proof, pause for the price reveal
- **The "wait for it" promise:** "in a minute I'll show you X" — and deliver it

## Risk Flags and Claim Safety

VSLs carry high claim risk because the claims are spoken, recorded, and often promoted. Flag for review:

- Income or earnings claims ("make $X," "replace your salary")
- Health, medical, or supplement claims
- Guaranteed results (without conditional language)
- Credential or award claims
- Comparative claims against named competitors
- Testimonial specificity that exceeds the source material

Use conditional language ("results vary," "typical results," "not a guarantee") where appropriate, and route risky claims to the client for legal review before recording.
