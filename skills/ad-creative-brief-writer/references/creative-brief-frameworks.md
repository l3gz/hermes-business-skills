# Creative Brief Frameworks Reference

This reference summarizes the frameworks used by the `ad-creative-brief-writer` skill. It is a field guide distilled from the indexed sources (Master Meta Ad Knowledge, Ad Script Writer, and VSL notebooks), covering format/aspect-ratio matrices by placement, the first-3-seconds patterns, text overlay rules and Meta policy, creative concept frameworks, and provider-specific generation parameter guides. Use it to structure the brief, then verify all claims, proof, prices, and visuals against the client's real offer, brand assets, and approved claims.

---

## 1. Format and Aspect-Ratio Matrix by Placement

Under Meta's modern architecture, the creative does the targeting — the algorithm reads the image, parses the copy, transcribes the video, and matches the creative to the right audience in real time. Format and aspect ratio determine where the creative can be delivered and how it reads on the screen. Producing only one ratio restricts placement reach; producing the wrong ratio for a placement looks broken.

### The Placement-to-Format Map

| Placement | Primary ratio | Acceptable | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Facebook Feed | 4:5 | 1:1, 16:9 | 4:5 takes the most mobile screen real estate and outperforms 1:1 on Feed. |
| Instagram Feed | 4:5 | 1:1, 16:9 | Same as Facebook Feed — 4:5 preferred. |
| Instagram Reels | 9:16 | — | Full-bleed vertical only. Letterboxed 1:1 looks broken. |
| Instagram Stories | 9:16 | — | Full-bleed vertical only. |
| Facebook Stories | 9:16 | — | Full-bleed vertical only. |
| Marketplace | 1:1 | 4:5 | Square is native to the catalog-style grid. |
| Video Feeds (in-stream) | 16:9 | 9:16, 1:1 | Horizontal preferred for in-stream; vertical for Reels-style. |
| Audience Network | 1:1 | 4:5, 16:9 | Square is the safest cross-placement fallback. |
| Right column (desktop) | 1:1 | 4:5 | Low-priority placement; square fallback. |

### The Safe-Zone Rule for 9:16 Video

Vertical video (9:16) is the recommended format for Reels, Stories, and cold-traffic scroll-stops. But 9:16 video delivered to the Feed is often cropped to 4:5 to fit the Feed frame. **Keep all key content — faces, text overlays, product hero shots, and CTAs — within a 4:5 safe zone** (the central horizontal band of the 9:16 frame). Anything above or below the 4:5 band risks being cut off on Feed delivery while displaying correctly on Reels/Stories.

### Format Selection by Objective

| Objective | Best format | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Cold-traffic scroll-stop | Vertical video 9:16 | Motion + sound + pattern interrupt earns the first 3 seconds. |
| Single benefit, fast iteration | Static image 4:5 | Cheap, fast to build, easy to iterate, outperforms video in high-CPM periods. |
| Retargeting warm traffic | Static image 4:5 or 1:1 | Warm audiences need a nudge, not a pitch. Statics convert at low CPM. |
| Multi-product / multi-feature story | Carousel 1:1 or 4:5 | One idea per card; lets the user self-select. |
| Founder-led explainer / long-form | Horizontal 16:9 or vertical 9:16 long-form | Long-form is an intentional format, not a padded short — use the time the message needs. |
| Catalog retargeting (DPA) | 1:1 (from product feed) | Automated; the feed provides the images. |

### Static vs Video vs Carousel — When Each Wins

- **Statics** sit lower in the funnel. They frequently outperform video during high-CPM periods (February, June–August) because they are shown directly to users with immediate purchasing behavior and waste fewer impressions. They allow rapid, knee-jerk decisions and are cheap and fast to build and iterate. Strong static types: benefit call-outs, us-versus-them tables, "ugly" ad statics, feature/problem-focused layouts, text-only stills, testimonials.
- **Video** is ideal for top-of-funnel demand generation, visual storytelling, product demos, and emotional connection. UGC, scripted UGC, and founder-led videos excel at capturing cold traffic and driving it down the funnel. Long-form explainers (1–10 minutes) work when the product requires it — "long-form" is an intentional format, not a strict duration.
- **Carousels** show multiple products, highlight specific features, or tell a quick multi-step story. Catalog ads (DPAs) are automated from the product feed and excel at retargeting warm site traffic.

## 2. The First 3 Seconds Framework (Video Briefs)

For any video creative, the first 3 seconds carry the entire ad. The first line of the primary copy represents ~90% of the ad's success — if it does not stop the scroll, nothing else matters. A high-converting video hook requires four synchronized elements:

### The Four Hook Elements

1. **Text overlay hook** — the burned-in headline. Short (≤20 chars), hyper-specific, readable on a muted phone. Pose a question or make a bold claim.
2. **Sound hook** — the first audio beat. A provocative question, a sound effect, a music drop. Assume 67% of mobile traffic is muted; the sound hook rewards those who unmute but must not be required to understand the ad.
3. **Visual hook** — the opening frame and the first movement. A pattern interrupt: an unexpected object close to the lens (Becker's USB drive), a contrasting color, a sudden motion, a whiteboard with handwritten math or logic.
4. **Vibe** — the emotional register of the opening. Urgent, conspiratorial, excited, deadpan, vulnerable. The vibe must match the avatar's awareness level.

### Tested Hook Styles

- **Stalker hooks:** hyper-contextual hooks that make the viewer feel you are stalking them through their phone or reading their thoughts. "You're getting booked calls from ads, but they show up unqualified with terrible show rates?"
- **Specific over generic:** avoid "Are your ads not making money?" Use the avatar's exact, hyper-specific pain language. Pull from the avatar profile.
- **Visual and verbal pattern interrupts:** props, text overlays, contrasting colors, unexpected movements in the first 3 seconds. A whiteboard with hand-written math acts as a powerful, authoritative visual hook.
- **Curiosity frames:** open with an unusual, mysterious visual object close to the lens to trigger an unanswered loop the viewer must resolve.
- **Bold bet / guarantee headlines:** "Your ads scale, or you don't pay." A high-stakes guarantee forces attention because the viewer assumes you could not make such a statement if it were not true.

### The 60-90-Second Structure

A standard direct-response video ad runs 60–90 seconds. Within it:

- **3–5 seconds:** the opening hook (the four elements above).
- **5–10 seconds:** the final CTA.
- **The remainder:** the body — the payoff of the hook, the mechanism, the proof.

Do not pad a 15-second message into a 60-second ad. If the message is simple, keep the ad short. Long ads with thin content inflate CPA and accelerate creative fatigue.

### Hook-to-Awareness Mapping

| Awareness level | Best hook style | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Unaware (cold) | Curiosity / contrarian / visual pattern interrupt | "The real reason your ads are failing isn't your creative; it's the 97% of buyers you're ignoring." |
| Problem-aware | Problem hooks — hyper-specific pain | "Booked calls from ads, but they show up unqualified?" |
| Solution-aware | Story / mechanism / contrarian | The epiphany bridge, the "new opportunity" framing. |
| Product-aware (hot) | Direct claim / big promise / guarantee | "Give me 7 days and I will place a proven appointment setter." |

## 3. Text Overlay Rules and Meta Policy

Text burned into an image or the first frame of a video is both a scroll-stop tool and a targeting signal. Meta's algorithm reads text overlays directly from the image file to help target the ideal audience. But Meta also historically penalizes images with excessive text.

### Length and Readability

- **Aim for ≤20 characters for the headline.** The overlay is the hook compressed; the full hook lives in the caption.
- **Large, bold, high-contrast, readable in 1 second on a muted phone.** If a user cannot read it instantly, it fails.
- **Pose a question or make a bold claim** that earns the click to "See More."

### Meta Text-in-Image Behavior

- Meta reads text overlays from the image for targeting.
- Historically, images with more than ~20% text coverage are penalized (lower reach, higher CPM). The hard threshold has softened under modern architecture, but the principle holds: minimal text, high contrast.
- Keep text off the subject's face and off the product hero shot.
- **Prefer text photographed in context over digitally overlaid text.** A post-it note, a letterboard, handwriting on a physical product, a whiteboard with handwritten math. In-context text reads as native content and avoids the over-text penalty. It also reads as more authentic on cold feeds.

### The "Scratch Out" Test

A strong creative must be unique to this brand. If you can scratch out the logo, write in a competitor's, and the ad still functions normally, it is not a strong creative — rework it. The creative concept must be inseparable from the brand's specific offer, avatar, and angle.

### The 10-Second Scroll Test

A user should be able to scroll through the ad copy and the visual for 10 seconds and instantly understand the value proposition, the pain, the credibility, and the offer. If the creative requires more than 10 seconds to communicate its core promise, it is overloaded.

## 4. Creative Concept Frameworks

The creative concept is the single visual idea the asset must communicate — the visual translation of the chosen hook. Choose the framework that matches the avatar's awareness level and the campaign objective.

### 1. Hero Product Shot

The product is the focal point, with benefits called out as overlays.

- **Best for:** product-aware and warm traffic; ecommerce; offers where the product's visual form is a selling point.
- **Visual structure:** product centered, clean background, 2–4 benefit call-outs as small overlays or arrows, brand logo subtly placed.
- **Overlay:** the single biggest benefit or the bold guarantee.
- **Risk:** can read as a catalog photo if the overlay is not scroll-stopping. Pair with a hyper-specific pain headline.

### 2. Lifestyle Context

The product in the avatar's real environment, showing the desired outcome in use.

- **Best for:** problem-aware traffic; offers where the transformation is contextual (fitness, home, food, B2B workflow).
- **Visual structure:** the avatar-proxy using the product in a recognizable setting, the outcome visible (the clean desk, the fit body, the booked calendar).
- **Overlay:** the outcome stated as the avatar's desire.
- **Risk:** can feel generic if the setting is stocky. Use the avatar's specific environment, not a stock-photo set.

### 3. UGC Style (Raw / Authentic)

Raw, smartphone-authentic, "real person discovers" framing.

- **Best for:** cold traffic; feeds where polished ads trigger ad-blindness; offers where social proof drives the click.
- **Visual structure:** smartphone framing, vertical 9:16, person talking to camera or demonstrating, minimal production value, captions burned in.
- **Overlay:** the person's first-person hook ("I didn't believe this would work...").
- **Risk:** must feel genuinely raw, not staged-raw. Over-produced UGC reads as fake and underperforms.

### 4. Problem Visualization

Show the pain vividly before the solution.

- **Best for:** problem-aware traffic; offers where the pain is visual (mess, clutter, waste, failure).
- **Visual structure:** the "before" state as the hero — the overflowing inbox, the messy desk, the wasted ad spend dashboard, the before photo.
- **Overlay:** the pain in the avatar's exact language.
- **Risk:** can feel negative if the solution is not implied or shown in the CTA beat. Always pair with a clear path to the resolution.

### 5. Transformation Reveal

The before-and-after split, the result made visible.

- **Best for:** solution-aware traffic; offers where proof drives the click (fitness, coaching, design, renovation).
- **Visual structure:** split frame (before | after), or a sequence reveal (problem → mechanism → result).
- **Overlay:** the result as a quantifiable claim ("From 0 to 50 clients in 90 days").
- **Risk:** before-and-after claims in regulated verticals (health, finance, income) carry legal risk. Flag for review.

### 6. Authority / Proof Stack

Testimonial screenshots, logos, stats burned in as the hero visual.

- **Best for:** product-aware and retargeting traffic; B2B and high-ticket offers where credibility is the bottleneck.
- **Visual structure:** a grid or stack of real testimonials, recognizable client logos, or a single punchy stat as the hero.
- **Overlay:** the single most powerful proof point.
- **Risk:** only works with real proof. Never fabricate testimonials, logos, or stats. Flag missing proof.

## 5. Provider-Specific Generation Parameter Guides

The creative brief must translate into the parameters the chosen provider needs. This is what makes the brief "generation-ready." Below are the parameter guides for the common providers.

### Higgsfield (Premium Video)

**When to use:** high-production vertical video where motion, camera moves, and polish matter. Premium cost per generation.

**Parameters to specify:**

- **Preset / template:** the Higgsfield preset that matches the desired motion (cinematic, dynamic, static-camera, product-spin).
- **Motion intensity:** low (subtle), medium, high (energetic). Match to the vibe from the first-3-seconds framework.
- **Camera move:** dolly-in, pan, orbit, push-in, static. Specify for the opening frame and the body.
- **First-frame reference:** provide a reference image or a detailed description of the opening frame — Higgsfield generates from the first frame.
- **Duration:** 5s, 10s, 15s. Match to the ad length.
- **Aspect ratio:** 9:16 for Reels/Stories; 16:9 for horizontal; 1:1 for square.
- **Style modifier:** photorealistic, cinematic, animated, minimal.
- **Number of variants:** 3–5 for a test batch.

**Strengths:** motion quality, camera moves, production polish. **Weaknesses:** cost per generation; weaker for static images; text rendering is limited — plan to add overlays in post.

### Nano Banana / fal.ai (Quality at Cost)

**When to use:** quality image and short-video generation at a lower cost than Higgsfield. Strong for concept exploration and static generation.

**Parameters to specify:**

- **Model id:** the specific fal.ai model (e.g., the Nano Banana image model, or a video model).
- **Prompt:** the full visual concept from the brief — scene, subject, composition, mood, color, texture.
- **Aspect ratio:** match to placement.
- **Style:** photorealistic, illustrated, UGC-raw, cinematic, minimal.
- **Number of variants:** 3–5 for a test batch; more for concept exploration.
- **Negative prompts:** text artifacts, extra fingers, watermark, distorted faces, logo duplication.
- **Seed:** if iterating a winner, fix the seed and vary one parameter.

**Strengths:** cost-to-quality ratio; fast iteration; good for statics. **Weaknesses:** text rendering is limited — plan to add overlays in post; motion is less polished than Higgsfield for video.

### OpenAI Images (Cheap, Simple)

**When to use:** fast, simple static creatives; concept exploration at low cost. Best for briefs that do not require fine text rendering or complex motion.

**Parameters to specify:**

- **Model:** the OpenAI image model (e.g., gpt-image-1, dall-e-3).
- **Prompt:** the full visual concept.
- **Size:** 1024×1024 (1:1), 1024×1792 (vertical, ~9:16), 1792×1024 (horizontal, ~16:9). Note: OpenAI's vertical is not a perfect 9:16 — plan to crop or pad for exact placement ratios.
- **Quality:** standard or high-definition.
- **Style:** natural or vivid.
- **Number of variants:** 1 per call (generate multiple calls for a batch).

**Strengths:** cheap, fast, simple, good prompt adherence. **Weaknesses:** text rendering is unreliable — do not rely on OpenAI Images to render the overlay text; generate the visual and add the overlay in post (Figma, Canva, or code). Limited to static images.

### Local sd.cpp (Free, Full Control)

**When to use:** the client wants full control, no API cost, and custom-trained models (a brand-specific LoRA, a product checkpoint). Best when the client has a local GPU or a dedicated generation box.

**Parameters to specify:**

- **Checkpoint / model:** the base model (e.g., SDXL, a photorealism checkpoint) and any LoRA (brand-specific, style-specific).
- **Sampler:** DPM++ 2M Karras, Euler a, etc. (DPM++ 2M Karras is a strong default for photorealism).
- **Steps:** 20–40 (higher = more refined, slower).
- **CFG scale:** 5–9 (higher = more prompt-adherent, can over-saturate; 7 is a common default).
- **Dimensions:** match to placement (e.g., 832×1216 for 9:16 vertical on SDXL; 1024×1024 for 1:1).
- **Prompt:** the full visual concept, plus style modifiers (e.g., "RAW photo, 8k uhd, dslr, soft lighting, high quality").
- **Negative prompt:** "text, watermark, signature, extra fingers, deformed, ugly, blurry, low quality, cartoon, 3d render" (tailor to the brief).
- **Seed:** fix for iteration; vary for exploration.
- **Number of variants:** batch size per run (e.g., batch of 4–8).

**Strengths:** free at generation time; full control; custom LoRAs for brand consistency; reproducible via seed. **Weaknesses:** requires local GPU and setup; text rendering is poor — add overlays in post; slower iteration than API providers unless well-provisioned.

### Human Designer / Editor

**When to use:** when text legibility and brand-perfect typography are critical; when the creative requires sourcing real assets (product photos, testimonial screenshots); when the cut and sound design of a video matter more than the generation.

**Parameters to specify (the designer brief):**

- **Scene description:** the full visual concept.
- **Assets to source:** product photos, testimonial screenshots, brand logo, brand font, brand colors.
- **Overlays to typeset:** the exact headline (≤20 chars), supporting text, CTA, in the brand font.
- **Composition and layout:** framing, where the eye lands, safe zone for 9:16.
- **Cut and sound design (video):** the beat sheet, the music direction, the caption style.
- **Deliverables:** file format, dimensions, number of variants.

**Strengths:** handles fine text and brand typography that AI tools cannot; sources real assets; controls the cut and sound. **Weaknesses:** slower and more expensive than generation; does not scale for high-variant testing.

## 6. Variant Planning for Creative Testing

When briefing a batch of creative variants for a test (see `meta-creative-tester`), structure the briefs to isolate one variable. The variables, in order of testing impact:

1. **Hook** — the first 3 seconds (video) or the first line / overlay (static). ~90% of the ad's success. Highest leverage.
2. **Format** — video vs static vs carousel. Never mix formats in one testing ad set.
3. **Angle** — the core message / pain point / desire.
4. **Offer framing** — how the offer is positioned (free book vs application call vs direct purchase).
5. **Length** — 15s vs 30s vs 60s+.
6. **CTA** — the call to action and its framing.

### Batch Structure Rules

- **One variable per batch.** Every brief in the batch shares all other parameters; only the tagged variable changes.
- **3–5 briefs per batch.** Fewer than 3 tests nothing; more than 6 splits budget too thin to reach 50 conversions each.
- **Never mix static and video in the same batch.** The delivery algorithm treats formats differently; mixing ruins the read.
- **Include a control.** If there is a known winner, one brief is the control; the variants are measured against it.

### The 70/20/10 Iteration Cycle

For the batch after a winner graduates (Hormozi's framework):

- **70% slight variations of the winner:** different clothing, horizontal flip, minor color filter, same hook and body. Extends the winner's life.
- **20% decent variations:** new hook, different spokesperson, different setting, same angle.
- **10% brand-new concepts:** new angle, new format, new concept framework. Discovers the next winner.

Tag each brief in the batch with its role (control / 70% slight / 20% decent / 10% new concept).

## 7. Message Match to the Destination

The creative and the landing page must match on three pillars. A broken message match is a primary cause of funnel drop-off.

- **Aesthetic match:** visual styling, color, design atmosphere. If the creative is raw UGC and the landing page is a polished corporate site, the click feels broken.
- **Language match:** tone, terminology, key phrases. If the creative uses the avatar's pain language, the page must use the same language.
- **Promise match:** the core hook and promised outcome in the creative is the primary focus of the page. If the creative promises a specific quantifiable result ("30 to 50 high-ticket clients"), the page must lead with that exact outcome.

Flag any disconnect between the creative and the destination page in the brief's `Needs Confirmation` section.

## 8. Creative Lifecycle and Fatigue (Context for Briefing)

Creatives on Meta have a volatile lifespan: launch → learning → peak → fatigue → refresh. When briefing a batch, the brief should be aware of where the account is in the lifecycle.

- **Fatigue signals:** frequency above 5 (cold) or 5–8 (retargeting), CTR dropping, CPM rising, CPA rising.
- **Refresh cadence:** plan to rotate or refresh active creatives every 2–4 weeks. Have the next batch ready before the current winner fatigues.
- **The iterate-losers trap:** Meta's Lattice infrastructure clusters similar-looking ads by Entity ID. Minor tweaks (background color, small headline swap) to a losing creative do not revive it. To iterate a winner, build conceptually diverse new visuals around the winning message. To recover a loser, start over with a new concept.

When briefing a refresh, do not brief minor tweaks to a fatiguing creative — brief a conceptually new creative around the winning message.
